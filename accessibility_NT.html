Why?

Founded on the simple and enduring idea that people need historic, beautiful and natural places (National Trust, 2014), National England is a conversation organisation, who preserve the nation’s heritage and open spaces for everyone to enjoy. Looking after over 500 historic houses, castles, and ancient monuments garden, the National Trust saw more than 20 million visits (National Trust, 2014).

According to the National Trust. historic buildings offer a connection to history, as well as a different perspective on the world around us (National Trust, 2014). Therefore,I wanted to explore whether there were any areas of England that were less accessible to a National Trust house.

Background and Methodology

As defined by Guers and van Wee (2004, p.128), accessibility is the “extent to which land-use and transport systems enable individuals to reach activities or destinations”.

Although there are multiple methods for assessing accessibility (see Guers and van Eck, 2001; Talen, 2003 for a methodological review), this study utilises the Cumulative opportunity measure (e.g., handy and Niemeiere, 1997; Vickerman, 1974). Simply, the cumulative opportunity approach sums the number of essential destinations reachable within a certain time by automobiles (Golub and Martens, 2014).

In this case, the origin points will be the National trust houses, each with a drive time of 60 minutes. The essential destinations will be the location of output area centroids, with the values summed being the total population within the output areas that can be reached within the time.

By doing this, this study aims to identify spatial gaps. In other words, where there are output area centroids (and therefore populations) that are further than 60 minutes drive time from any National Trust house.

Datasets

Due to the location of National Trust houses not being publically available within a dataset, this study utilises Overpass Turbo, a web-based data filtering tool for Open Street Map to extract all buildings that have a value of “National Trust” within the “operator” key. 205 Buildings were extracted, providing 82% of the buildings that the National Trust look after (249).

The English Census Output Areas, 2011 were downloaded from UK Data Service whilst the 2011 Census Population Density data (QS102EW) was downloaded from nomis

Isochrone Generation

nt_houses_c <- gCentroid(nt_houses, byid = TRUE)

nt_houses_c <- SpatialPointsDataFrame(nt_houses_c, as.data.frame(nt_houses$Name))

nt_houses_c <- spTransform(nt_houses_c, "+proj=longlat +ellps=WGS84 +datum=WGS84 +no_defs")

nt_houses_c_eng <- nt_houses_c[!is.na(over(nt_houses_c, eng_outline)),]

plot(eng_outline)
points(nt_houses_c, col = "red")
points(nt_houses_c_eng, col = "blue")

isolist <- list()

for (i in 1:length(nt_houses_c_eng)){
  while(TRUE){
    iso <- try(osrmIsochrone(loc = nt_houses_c_eng[i,], breaks = seq(from = 0,to = 60, by = 60)))
    if(!is(iso, 'try-error')) break
  }
  isolist[[i]] <- iso
}

isos <- do.call(bind, isolist)

drivetime_extent <- unionSpatialPolygons(isos, ID = isos$id)
plot(drivetime_extent)
plot(nt_houses_c_eng, add = T, col = "red")

Future Developments

  • Scrape National Trust house locations from National Trust website and geocode, instead of using Open Street Map.

  • Create an ‘opportunity score’ by getting the ratio of population density to National Trust houses for each output area centroid. This will allow for comparison amongst output areas.

  • The Cumulative opportunity method assumes that only National Trust houses within 60 minutes drive time are accessible; the method does not consider that the drive time may vary depending on the National Trust house. Therefore, other accessibility measures should be explored, such as Utility Based measures (Handy and Niemeier, 1997).
